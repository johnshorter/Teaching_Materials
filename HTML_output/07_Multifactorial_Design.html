What is a multifactorial design?

A multifactorial design is an experiment that have multiple factors of independent variables. It might seem a bit abstract. So lets see an actual example.

We will use the R built-in dataset ToothGrowth as an example. ToothGrowth is an experiment in which researchers looked at the effect of two supplements VC and OJ on the tooth length of guinea pigs.

Each animal was given a supplement (either OJ or VC); and the animal can receive a high dose (2), mid dose (1) or low dose (0.5). At the end of the experiment, the longest tooth of the animal is measured. Ref: Crampton, E. W. (1947). The Journal of Nutrition, 33(5), 491504. doi: 10.1093/jn/33.5.491.

##  $ dose: num  0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 ...

Looks like it has 60 rows and 3 columns. len is tooth length. It will be our dependent variable. There are 2 kinds of supp: VC or OJ. And there are multiple levels of dose, which is recorded as a numeric variable in the table.

This experiment is called a multifactorial design because it has two independent variables of interest: supp and dose.

supp is a factor; dose is another factor. We have two factors of interest, thus multifactorial.

Why would one want to do a multifactorial experiment?

The multifactorial design is popular because it is efficient. When we have multiple independent variables of interest, it allows us to compare multiple combinations of independent variables in the same time.

Lets use the ToothGrowth data as an example. There are two supplements and three doses of each. 2 * 3 = 6 combinations. And the multifactorial design will allow us to compare all 6 combinations in the same time.

What is interaction among factors?

A very important concept of the multifactorial design is interaction. Interaction stands for the effect of one factor depends on the other factor.

To understand this, lets visualize the data first.

You can see that, as the dose of either supplement increases, the teeth length also increases. So for both supplements, higher dose = longer teeth.

But if you look carefully at the lines, you will notice that the two curves are not exactly parallel. While teeth are longer for OJ than VC in lower doses, there is no difference in dose = 2.

And THAT is interaction. The effect of supplement depends on the dose. The effect of one factor (supplement) depends on the other factor (dose). Thats interaction. In the graphical sense, it means the trends are not all parallel to each other.

When you set up a multifactorial design, you will most certainly encounter interactions.

How to analyze a multifactorial experiment?

You will analyze a multifactorial experiment like a randomized block experiment, except that you will have to consider interaction now.

Lets fill out the table.

source factor levels
supp treat 2
dose treat 3
animal OU, EU 60

We have supp and dose as our treatments. They have 2 and 3 levels, respectively. We have 60 observations of animal (each dot on the graph is an animal). In this case the animal is both observational units and experimental units.

Last time we learned that we should include all sources of variation except OU. So that gives len ~ supp + dose.

But how do I include the interaction between supp and dose in my linear model?

Setting up linear model for multifactorial design

To include an interaction in a linear model, you use the factor1:factor2 syntax.

That is len ~ supp + dose + supp:dose

There is a different syntax that you may also see for interactions. A simplified syntax is len ~ supp * dose. The * symbol means + and interaction, that is supp * dose = supp + dose + supp:dose

Oftentimes people refer to the independent variables as the main effects. So supp and dose are your main effects.

When you set up your model, you type in the main effects first, then the interactions.

WARNING: the dose is recorded as a numeric variable in the dataset. In a multifactorial experiment, we need to set it as factor.

ToothGrowth_new <- ToothGrowth %>% 
  mutate(dose.f = as.factor(dose))

We can pull out the summary statistics first: In this case we will group by both supp and dose.f, the two factors.

ToothGrowth_new %>% 
  group_by(supp, dose.f) %>% 
  summarise(mean = mean(len),
            var = var(len),
            sd = sd(len),
            n = n())

Well, the variances are not ideal, as not all of them are within a two-fold range, but all of them are at least within an order of magnitude. So maybe itll be okay.

model_teeth <- lm(len ~ supp + dose.f + supp:dose.f, data = ToothGrowth_new)

You can also do len ~ supp * dose.f. That gives you the same thing.

Note that you can also set it up as len ~ dose.f * supp. Technically there is a minute difference between the two models, but oftentimes it makes no difference to your conclusions at the end of the day. We will ignore that technicality for now.

Lets check the assumptions of ANOVA first. Normality?

plot(model_teeth, which = 2)

Pretty good!

Next, equal variance?

plot(model_teeth, which = c(1, 3))

  1. supp has a F >> 1. That means the means of supp are different

  2. dose.f has F >> 1 but also larger than the F value of supp. This means not only are the means of dose.f different, but also dose.f explain a larger proportion of variation in the data than supp.

  3. Lastly, the interaction supp:dose.f has an F value significantly > 1 (p = 0.02 < 0.05). This means the effect of supp depends on the dose, consistent with what the graph showed us.

Now we can go ahead and find out which supp:dose combination gave us the longest teeth.

Tukey tests

When you do a Tukey test on a multifactorial experiment, You need to be VERY THOUGHTFUL. Ask yourself what is the comparison that I am actually interested in?

In this example: Am I interested in the effect of supp at each dose level? Am I interested in the effect of dose for each supp?

Depending on the question of interest, you will need a different Tukey test layout. Of course the choice of analysis all depends on the researcher and their interests.

Test effect of supp at each dose level

Lets say we are interested in the effect of supp at each dose level. To specify that in the emmeans() command, we will use the ~ supp | dose syntax. This means compare across supp at each dose level.

estimate_per_dose <- emmeans(model_teeth,pairwise ~ supp | dose.f)

Test effect of dose at each supp

Now lets say we are interested in the effect of different doses for each supp. How should we set up the emmeans() command?

Youll use ~ dose.f | supp now. This means compare across dose at each supp level.

estimate_per_supp <- emmeans(model_teeth,pairwise ~ dose.f | supp) 
estimate_per_supp$contrast

This tells us that

  1. For OJ, dose 0.5 is significantly lower than dose 1 and 2. Dose 2 is not significantly different from dose 1.

  2. For VC, as the dose increases, each dose is significantly different from the previous.

Hopefully you can see that depending on how we set up the emmeans command, we get different inferences. So before you set up a Tukey test for a multifactorial experiment, do think about what you are actually interested in.
